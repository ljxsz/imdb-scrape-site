Rain Man is a film that, on paper, could have easily slipped into the kind of saccharine melodrama that Hollywood so often indulges in when handling sensitive subject matter. And yet, somehow, it manages to rise far above that. Despite being a product of the late 80s and undeniably showing its age in places-particularly in its portrayal of autism-it remains a deeply moving, surprisingly funny, and profoundly human story that hit me harder than I expected. What truly anchors this journey is the performances. Much has been said about Dustin Hoffman's portrayal of Raymond, and rightfully so-his performance is measured, consistent, and rich with subtlety. But for me, the revelation was Tom Cruise. It's easy to forget, with his reputation for blockbuster spectacle, how good Cruise can be when he's given something to chew on. His portrayal of Charlie Babbitt isn't instantly likable, and that's what makes it work. There's an edge to him, a selfishness that softens not through some grand epiphany, but through gradual exposure to the quiet presence of his brother. It's a character arc that feels earned rather than imposed. I've never had a brother, but that didn't stop the emotional current of this movie from pulling me in. There's something universal in the idea of confronting your own selfishness, in being forced to care for someone when you don't quite know how. Their journey-awkward, frustrating, and full of unspoken understanding-resonated deeply. It's a road movie, yes, but more than that, it's a film about reconnection. About family rediscovered, even if that family never quite looks the way you expected. The soundtrack is another highlight, full of evocative tones that give the film a cool, rhythmic momentum. It's got that road-trip vibe, yes, but there's something haunting under the surface too-a quiet ache that matches Raymond's detached presence and Charlie's simmering frustration. And what really surprised me was how funny the film often is. Not in a forced, gag-driven way, but through character and situation. Raymond's blunt honesty leads to some brilliant moments, and Cruise's reactions only heighten the humor. That said, the film isn't without its stumbles. As time has passed, the accuracy of its portrayal of autism has rightfully been questioned. It's a narrow depiction, and while it served the story in 1988, it now feels overly simplistic in some ways. And the pacing, especially as the film winds toward its conclusion, does begin to feel sluggish. There's a point where you understand what the film wants to say, and yet it keeps lingering, perhaps unsure of how to gracefully close the curtain. Still, these are minor blemishes on what is otherwise a deeply affecting experience. Rain Man is not just about autism, or Vegas, or flashy cars-it's about the space between people, the barriers we build, and the quiet bridges we can form if we just listen. It's not perfect. It's not always accurate. But it's sincere, and sometimes that's more than enough.

Hollywood always believed that if a movie dealt with a serious theme in an entertaining manner it was somehow worthy of consideration which is why so many second-rate films like "Rain Man" ended up winning the Academy Award for Best Picture. As 'Best Pictures' go "Rain Man" wasn't quite the worst of them but you can't help feeling it trivializes its subject which is played almost entirely for comedy or sentimental value. If it's enjoyable, and in its way it is, it is due entirely to the two principal performances of Dustin Hoffman, (an Oscar for best actor), and Tom Cruise, (no nomination, but thoroughly deserving of one). They play estranged brothers reunited after the death of their father. Hoffman is an autistic-savant, (definitely qualifying as a handicap; definitely several rungs up the Oscar ladder), whose facility with numbers proves a definite plus when it comes to playing the tables in Las Vegas. (The journey from first meeting to final parting also qualifies this as a road-movie). Cruise is the initially venal younger brother who wants to use Hoffman's 'talents' for his own ends but, this being a Hollywood movie of the inspirational variety, it isn't too long before bonding takes place and at the time Cruise's persona was such that playing a bad guy wasn't really on the cards. If anyone ever doubted in the early days that Cruise could act all they had to do was to look to this and "The Colour of Money" where he gave his Oscar-winning co-stars as good as he got. The film's director was Barry Levenson, formerly as script-writer who graduated to making his own films with the marvelous "Diner" and whose best films tended towards autobiographical pieces set in his native Baltimore. "Rain Man" represented his attempt at tackling a 'big' subject. It had all the ingredients for popular success and it gave him the Oscar but it's still a bland, formulaic film typical of what the big studios were churning out at the time.
