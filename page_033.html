Jason Statham and director David Ayer reunite for A Working Man, following their success with The Beekeeper- another adrenaline-fueled ride. This time, they dive into the dark world of human trafficking, and the result is a relentless action thriller that pulls no punches. In his usual no-nonsense fashion, Statham dishes out swift and brutal justice, making every fight sequence feel personal and punishing. Scripted by Sylvester Stallone and based on Chuck Dixon's Levon's Trade (the first of 12 excellent, fast-paced books which I recommend - they are short novels with lots of action), the film sets the stage for what could be a long-running franchise. Statham's character, Levon Cade, is a former black ops operative trying to live a quiet life- until evil finds him first. One of the film's most memorable moments comes when Cade warns a trafficker, "You had your chance to walk away. Now I take everything." Moments later, the audience is treated to a bone-snapping, full-throttle takedown that leaves no doubt: he means it. Ayer's signature grit and Stallone's old-school action sensibilities make A Working Man more than just another Statham beatdown; it's a raw, relentless tale of vengeance and justice. If this is only the beginning, action fans should buckle up for the ride ahead. Don't listen to the naysayers; this is a great old-school meets new-school action popcorn flick.

Former Royal Marine Commando Levon Cade (Jason Statham) is now living in Chicago, Illinois working construction as he tries to be a good father to his daughter Meredith "Merry" (Isla Gie) who is in the primary custody of Levon's father-in-law Dr. Jordan Roth (Richard Heap) who holds Levon a grudge after the suicide of his daughter and Levon's wife during his time on deployment. When Jenny Garcia (Arianna Rivas), the daughter of Levon's boss and friend Joe (Michael Pena) is abducted while celebrating with friends, Joe enlists Levon's help in tracking her down sending Levon on a journey into the criminal underworld and on a collision course with the Russian mob outfit known as the Bratva. A Working Man reteams Jason Statham and director/co-writer David Ayer after the two worked together on the 2024 sleeper hit The Beekeeper. Adapted from the 2014 novel Levon's Trade written by comic writer Chuck Dixon (sort of like a cut rate Frank Miller with a similar downward career trajectory), the rights to the book were acquired by credited co-writer Sylvester Stallone's Balboa Productions with the initial plan to adapt the book and character to television. In 2023, the project was retooled as a feature film with Statham set to star and the project was acquired by Amazon MGM Studios two weeks after The Beekeeper opened to surprise critical and financial success. While A Working Man follows the well-trodden ground of vigilante trash pictures of the 80s, A Working Man mostly provides the over the top goofiness of The Exterminator or Death Wish 3 more than the ugliness of Kinjite: Forbidden Subjects or Death Wish II. As many have noted, Statham's archetypal action hero roles don't have much differentiation mainly falling into the men of few words who follow a strict moral code until pushed to action (such as Wrath of Man, Beekeeper, Mechanic, etc.). On occasion you'll see Statham touch into a more sardonic wiseass side of his personality with certain Guy Ritchie roles or the Crank films, but has mostly embraced his typecasting as a steady stream of work and certainly delivers the goods when it comes to action. When it comes to the direction, the film is less in line with David Ayer's End of Watch or Fury and more in line with the garish style of Suicide Squad or Beekeeper. Combined with a script co-written by Sylvester Stallone that's less Rocky and more the overly blunt Cobra or Homefront and when you couple Stallone's written lack of nuance with Ayer's directorial lack of nuance and mined from a source who has never known nuance you get some similar action excess that falls well within what was given to us with last year's Beekeeper. I will say while the style is as over the top as Beekeeper, the action premise is very standard vigilante fare which actually does work to its favor by having more varied and gritty action sequences where Statham's character doesn't have the nigh invincibility of his Beekeeper character meaning there's slightly more tension to the action sequences. A Working Man delivers exactly what you'd expect given the star and production team. With absolutely ridiculous action excess delivered with complete seriousness, it makes a welcome addition to other required viewings of the vigilante trash genre like The Exterminator or Death Wish 3.
