MAN ON THE RUN (2026) - Morgan Neville's documentary is a very personal look at Paul McCartney in the 1970s. It's not the "Wings" doc that some have dismissed it as, although, of course, that band figures prominently in McCartney's journey during the era. MAN ONTHE RUN is essentially bracketed by the release of his 1970 solo album MCCARTNEY which signified the end of The Beatles and the subsequent MCCARTNEY II in 1981 which signaled the end of Wings. Neville does a good job of assembling the interview subjects (all off camera) and clips of those who have departed such as Linda Eastman McCartney and John Lennon. Sean Lennon in particular carries a lot of weight here representing his late father. A shame that Ringo wasn't interviewed and is only represented by some very old archival clips. The main focus is on Paul and his relationship with Linda and his family more than the music per se. Neville's thesis here is that McCartney truly needed to get back to the egg after The Beatles broke up. He left London and went off to a Scottish farm. After a couple of excellent solo albums (which, surprisingly, weren't well-received at the time) he slowly worked his way into a new band. Wings was really Paul and Denny Laine, with Linda there more as moral and emotional support than as a musician. It featured a rotating bunch of guitarists and drummers over the decade a so of its existence (one of McCartney's best quips is equating Wings with Spinal Tap what with five different drummers - six, really, as Paul did almost all of it on BAND ON THE RUN). The one true exception was the talented guitarist Jimmy McCulloch, who sadly couldn't overcome his vices. The band should very much be remembered as "Paul McCartney and Wings." McCartney is interviewed so extensively, he essentially serves as the narrator. Neville does cover his drug arrests, disputes with the other Beatles and payment and credit issues with the other group members, but it doesn't dig too deeply in the dirt. It's an affirmative Doc, and a highly entertaining one.

The documentary about Paul McCartney's private life and musical career focuses on the period between the end of The Beatles and the death of John Lennon. In other words, it mainly centers on Wings, in its various lineups. Everything consists of real footage from that time, both amateur and professional recordings. The voice overs are taken from archival interviews with people who are no longer with us, and the film does not shy away from criticism. For me, this makes it a very powerful format. As a viewer, you are completely immersed in those years, it feels present and immediate. I much prefer this approach over many modern music documentaries, which often rely on a lineup of celebrities sitting in a studio, explaining how special, unique, and amazing it was to work with the star in question. This feels authentic and unfiltered, full of both joy and sadness, and packed with outstanding music. Definitely a film I want to watch again, perhaps more than once. I have rediscovered Wings, and I now have the live album Wings Over America on repeat on Spotify. Highly recommended.

"Paul McCartney: Man on the Run" is less a standard rock documentary and more a tender, pastoral therapy session disguised as a movie about Wings. It focuses on the messy, unglamorous aftermath of the Beatles, following Paul and Linda into the countryside as they build a new life out of mud, music, and quiet routines. The editing is spectacular and immersive, stitching together archival footage and home movies into something so gorgeous it almost feels unreal, as if some shots had been subtly reimagined rather than simply restored. What emerges is a very beautiful film about a family pushing through depression and grief by turning their private life into a shared artistic project. The documentary doesn't dodge the contradictions that make McCartney such a fascinating subject: the benevolent bandleader who is also a control freak, the man who insists Wings is "a real band" while everyone knows they're essentially backing an ex-Beatle with a songbook that bends gravity. Bringing Linda-far from a virtuoso-into the group, using Wings as his support system, and steering everything with his own towering ego would be damning in almost anyone else; in Paul's case it plays like a flawed but honest survival strategy. There's a poignant psychological undercurrent in the way both Lennon and McCartney, post-breakup, pull their partners into their music as if trying to replace a missing creative half, turning marriage into both life raft and band membership. The film may not offer shocking revelations, but it delivers a warm, intelligent, and emotionally satisfying portrait of how one man survived being Paul McCartney.

The Lost City has pieces that work and pieces that don't. Placing pretenders into an actual adventure is good for laughs and heart, yet much of this script is generic. Unimaginative MacGuffins, flat villains, and predictable romances are individually forgivable, but together, they override the premise's charm. Tatum's charisma shines through and Pitt's cameo provides flavor, but that isn't enough to pull The Lost City out of mediocrity. It isn't painful, but The Lost City misses more than it hits. Meanwhile, The Lost City's filmmaking is average. Its minor positives are the cinematography (intermittently dramatizing comedy and action), the sound (selling the combat and jungle setting), the production design (primarily real but often computerized), and the effects (employing real explosions amongst the CGI). Conversely, the editing occasionally sags (lingering on unessential jokes) and the music is distractingly trendy (forcing fun, rather than serving the narrative). Lastly, The Lost City lacks cohesion. Its humor and emotions never tonally blend, and instead take turns (which disconnects viewers). Ultimately, The Lost City has mild appeal, but functions inconsistently. Mainstream comedies that actually make it to theatres are slim to none these days. I remember a time when there was possibly a big comedy coming out every one or two months, with at least half of them being good, but in today's climate, humour is just tough to get right. I was very happy to see that Paramount would be taking a chance and releasing a big comedy in The Lost City this year, but I truly didn't know to what to expect from it. After seeing it in theatres, I'm happy to say that I had a good time with it. It's not great, but it felt like a throwback comedy, just with a modern twist. To put it simply, The Lost City follows Loretta (Sandra Bullock) as she's on a book tour with her cover model Alan (Channing Tatum) to promote the release of "The Lost City of D," but they are swept up in a kidnapping and find themselves on a real adventure in the jungle. Abigail Fairfax (Daniel Radcliffe) is the reason Loretta was kidnapped in the first place, as he sees many similarities in her book to that of a real lost city. Being the privileged twerp that he is makes for a fun villainous storyline, even if it's pretty lame at times. Radcliffe aside (whom I still enjoyed), this film puts all of its weight on Tatum and Bullock to deliver the goods. This premise is clever enough to work as a film, but it all comes down to who you cast and if they can make the material work. I never thought I needed to see this duo together, but I'm very happy they worked so well. On top of that, I have to admit that the best moments in the film feature Brad Pitt's character Jack Trainer. He isn't in the film much, but every second he was on screen had me laughing. Where I found the film to be slightly uneven though, was in the fact that I wasn't sure if it wanted to be funny or dramatic at times. I'm all for heartfelt storylines as well as comedy, but I would argue that the majority of the humour is in the first half, while the second half gets a little more sentimental. These both worked in the movie, but the way the film begins gives absolutely no foreshadowing as to what the film will become by the end. I found that odd, but again, the cast sold all of the dialogue for me. From a few hilarious moments to some fun adventure/action throughout, as I said, I had a good time with this one. In the end, The Lost City is exactly what I think moviegoers need right now. Nothing too serious or weighed down by heavy drama. This is a good old-fashioned, silly comedy/romance that I feel works for what it is. It's not going to be the type of comedy that a lot of people are going to watch over and over again, at least I don't think, but the heart of the film is in the right place and it never takes anything too seriously. Now playing in theatres, I'd give a recommendation to The Lost City.
